Five Traditions of
Sci-Fi Futures
Every civilisation tells stories about tomorrow. But the futures we imagine are not neutral — they are confessions. They reveal what we fear, what we believe we deserve, and whether we think we can do anything about it.
We analysed 6,877 works of science fiction from five cultural traditions. What we found was not a spectrum but a topology — five fundamentally different architectures of hope and dread.

// TRANSMISSION_001 — THE FRAMEWORK
Fred Polak’s
Image of the Future
In 1955, Dutch sociologist Fred Polak proposed something radical: that the health of a civilisation can be diagnosed by the stories it tells about the future. Not by their accuracy — but by their emotional architecture.
His framework maps two axes. Essence asks: is tomorrow better or worse than today? This is the mood of a culture’s imagination — its default emotional posture toward what comes next. Influence asks: can humans shape the future, or are we passengers? This is the agency question — whether a culture believes its own stories of resistance and transformation.
Together these axes create four quadrants — four fundamentally different stances a civilisation can take toward its own survival. We applied this framework to 6,877 works of science fiction across five cultural traditions. The results are not what you’d expect.

// TRANSMISSION_002 — THE DOMINANT VOICE
Western Tradition
5,350 works. Nearly 78% of the global corpus. When we say “sci-fi,” we almost always mean Western sci-fi — and that’s already the first finding. The genre’s centre of gravity is Anglo-American, and its gravitational pull has shaped every other tradition’s self-definition.
Western sci-fi is moderately pessimistic (Essence: -0.186) but believes in human agency (Influence: +0.237). This is the tradition of the reluctant hero — things are bad, probably getting worse, but one individual can make a difference. It is the Hero’s Journey on repeat: Alien, The Matrix, Interstellar, The Hunger Games.
The Western signature is a kind of productive anxiety. The future is threatening enough to generate narrative tension, but agency is high enough to sustain the fantasy that one person can save the world. This is not accidental — it maps precisely onto the liberal-individualist political imagination.
Why does the data look this way? Western sci-fi inherits Enlightenment individualism fused with the American frontier myth — the pioneer who transgresses boundaries and transforms wilderness into civilisation. The Promethean tech stance captures the belief that unbounded technological ambition, despite moral danger, is both inevitable and ultimately salvific. Heroic Struggle dominates because the genre assumes society is fundamentally corruptible but redeemable by exceptional agents. Counter-reading: The persistence of Heroic Struggle as the dominant frame might signal not optimism about agency but deep anxiety about institutional failure. The hero must fight precisely because ordinary social structures are assumed corrupt beyond collective repair.

// TRANSMISSION_003 — THE HIGHEST AGENCY
Japanese Tradition
1,196 works. Japan’s sci-fi tradition is the least pessimistic in the dataset — and this should surprise anyone who thinks of anime as relentlessly dark. The darkness is there, but it comes with a counterweight that no other tradition matches: the highest Providential score (9.5%) of any culture.
Where Western sci-fi puts a lone hero at the centre, Japanese sci-fi puts the group. The agency is collective. In Neon Genesis Evangelion, the hero is psychologically shattered — the mecha works anyway. In Ghost in the Shell, identity dissolves into networks. In Akira, the individual transcends but the city survives through collective endurance.
Japanese sci-fi encodes a fundamentally different theory of survival: the individual is fragile, the technology is ambivalent, but the collective can absorb what would destroy any single person. This is Mecha as philosophy — the body is weak, so we build shells. Not for conquest, but for persistence.
Why does the data look this way? Post-war transformation through redemptive duty: the trauma of absolute defeat (1945) and atomic destruction created a cultural contract wherein sacrifice and collective obligation toward technological futurity become the path to civilisational honour. The high Providential score reflects active faith that technology — managed by a cohesive collective — can restore purpose. Delegation of agency to AI/Tech mirrors wa (harmony): machines as extensions of group will, not individual ambition. Counter-reading: The delegation to AI/Tech agency could indicate not cultural values but a retreat from human responsibility — a projection of the desire for redemption onto machines precisely because human-centred agency was discredited by the catastrophe of imperial militarism.

// TRANSMISSION_004 — THE FRONTIER BUILDERS
Chinese Tradition
153 works — a small but rapidly growing corpus that tells us something extraordinary. Chinese sci-fi is the only tradition where the dominant myth is not the Hero’s Journey but The Frontier. This is civilisational ambition encoded as narrative.
Liu Cixin’s Three-Body Problem is the flagship, but the pattern runs deeper. Chinese sci-fi imagines futures where the challenge is not personal but planetary — and the response is not individual heroism but infrastructural mobilisation. Technology here is neither salvation nor damnation; it is a tool of nation-scale construction.
The Chinese tradition inverts the Western formula: where Hollywood asks “who will save us?”, Chinese sci-fi asks “what will we build?” This is not optimism — the Essence score is darker than the Western average. It’s something more interesting: a pessimism that still believes in engineering its way out.
Why does the data look this way? The Frontier myth reflects a continuous historical identity rooted in civilisational expansion — from Han Dynasty territorial absorption to the contemporary Belt and Road imagination. Technology appears as a tool of state-coordinated modernisation rather than individual transgression. The Individual Hero agency is paradoxical: exceptional figures serve civilisational continuity rather than disrupting it, marking a fundamental divergence from the Western adversarial stance. Confucian order-making — conquering territory and integrating it into a coherent project — replaces the American lawless frontier. Counter-reading: The retreat into Frontier mythology might represent not cultural confidence but anxiety. Chinese sci-fi writers invoke expansion myths precisely when contemporary society faces doubts about whether technology can sustain centralised governance through accelerating change.

// TRANSMISSION_005 — THE SYSTEM SPEAKS
Soviet Tradition
75 works. The smallest tradition — and the most philosophically distinct. Soviet sci-fi has the lowest Influence score in the dataset (+0.145), meaning it believes less than any other tradition that humans can shape the future. It is also the only tradition where the dominant Future Vision is Dystopian, not Ambiguous.
This is not hopelessness. It’s something more subtle: a literature built on the premise that futures emerge from systems, not from heroes. Tarkovsky’s Stalker doesn’t solve the Zone — it contemplates it. The Strugatsky brothers’ Roadside Picnic imagines alien artefacts as cosmic indifference. Lem’s Solaris gives us an ocean that thinks but cannot be understood.
Soviet sci-fi is the tradition of systemic determinism — the conviction that the universe operates on logics that dwarf individual will. When it is hopeful, it’s because the system might deliver something good. When it’s bleak, it’s because the system doesn’t care. Either way, you are not the protagonist.
Why does the data look this way? Soviet/Russian sci-fi inherits a dual philosophical burden: Russian Orthodox fatalism — the conviction that divine or cosmic will is inscrutable — overlaid with Marxist historical materialism, which insists that systemic forces, not individuals, drive change. The collapse of Soviet ideology didn’t liberate the imagination; it replaced one form of determinism with another. Post-Soviet economic devastation confirmed the pattern: futures arrive through forces beyond individual control. The Neutral tech stance reflects technology as just another domain of systemic entrapment. Counter-reading: The prominence of Dystopia and undetermined agency might not signal defeatism but intellectual honesty — a refusal of the comforting Western fantasy that one person can change the world. Russian sci-fi may be the most epistemologically rigorous tradition: it insists on the limits of human comprehension.

// TRANSMISSION_006 — THE TRAP
Korean Tradition
103 works. Korean sci-fi carries the most striking single statistic in the entire dataset: 39.8% Tragic Determinism — nearly double the Western rate. This is a literature of entrapment. The future is dark, and you cannot change it.
Think Snowpiercer, where revolution merely reproduces the system. Think The Host, where the monster is really institutional negligence. Think Parasite — not technically sci-fi, but encoding the same logic that pervades Korean speculative fiction: the architecture of inequality is load-bearing. You cannot remove it without collapsing the structure.
Korean sci-fi is the tradition that most directly confronts social class as a technology of control. Where Western sci-fi externalises its villains (aliens, AI, corporations), Korean sci-fi locates the enemy in the geometry of the system itself. The lowest Transformative score (5.8%) confirms it: this tradition has largely given up on utopia.
Why does the data look this way? The 1997 IMF crisis is the wound that organises Korean speculative fiction. It shattered the myth of Confucian capitalism — that shared sacrifice yields shared prosperity — and revealed a generation trapped in hierarchical structures designed for their exploitation. Confucian social hierarchy, historically legitimising order, was weaponised by neoliberal capital: traditional duty-based obligation meets contemporary precarity, producing narratives where escape is simultaneously necessary and impossible. The Hybrid Agency captures this precisely — characters have will but operate within predetermined constraints. Counter-reading: The Tragic Determinism reading might romanticise precarity. Korean sci-fi’s emphasis on entrapment could mask something more radical — that the genre itself has been captured by the systems it critiques, rendering the speculative imagination colonised by the very logic of inequality it depicts.

// TRANSMISSION_007 — WHAT THE MAP REVEALS
Five Ways to
Fear Tomorrow
The five traditions don’t form a hierarchy. They form a constellation — each illuminating a different failure mode of the human imagination. The West fears losing the individual. Japan fears losing the collective. China fears losing momentum. Russia fears the indifference of the cosmos. Korea fears the permanence of inequality.
What they share is more revealing than what divides them: every tradition is net-pessimistic. Not a single culture’s sci-fi imagines the future as, on balance, better than the present. The differences are in the flavour of that pessimism and the agency they grant their characters to fight it.
This is Polak’s deepest insight made empirical: the stories we tell about tomorrow are not predictions. They are diagnoses. And right now, across five distinct civilisations, the diagnosis is the same — the future is a problem. The only question is whether you’re the one solving it, surviving it, or trapped in it.
