§1 — The Architecture
Five layers. One decision spine.

Most insight systems fail because they're organised around research projects rather than the decisions they serve. This architecture inverts that — every layer feeds toward, and is accountable to, the decisions at its core.

1
Layer 01

Signal Capture

The raw nerve endings
Where observations enter the system before interpretation. The discipline is to record, not to conclude. Each signal is an atom — tagged but unprocessed.
↓ Click to expand

What enters here

  • Verbatims from research
  • Behavioural data spikes
  • Search trend anomalies
  • Social listening fragments
  • Competitor product changes
  • Cultural moments & events
  • Front-line staff observations
  • Customer service patterns

Signal taxonomy

  • Source type: Behavioural, attitudinal, cultural, competitive, category
  • Context signals: Background that shapes interpretation (Torres)
  • Behavioural signals: Actions that diverge from expected patterns
  • Confidence: Observed / Inferred / Speculative
  • Capture method: Qual, quant, passive, social, competitive intel
  • Relevance horizon: Immediate / Medium / Long
  • Fragment flag: Non-interview sources (tickets, analytics) are incomplete; treat as inspiration, not evidence

Rhythm

  • Continuous, always on
  • Low friction entry
  • Weekly interview cadence (Torres: "keystone habit")
  • Story-based capture: "Tell me about the last time you..."
  • Monthly signal digest

Key discipline

  • Capture now, act later: record what is interesting before it is actionable
  • Preserve original language
  • One signal per entry
  • Tag generously, filter later
  • Synthesise each interview separately before synthesising across (Torres)
2
Layer 02

Pattern Recognition

The connective tissue
Where individual signals converge. Three signals from different sources pointing in the same direction become a pattern. Patterns carry a convergence score and a trajectory.
↓ Click to expand

Pattern anatomy

  • Name: A descriptive, memorable label
  • Contributing signals: Linked from Layer 1
  • Convergence score: Independent source types that corroborate
  • Trajectory: Emerging → Accelerating → Peaking → Declining
  • Start mapping after 3–4 independent signals (Torres: prevents overreaction to one source and paralysis from waiting for dozens)

Convergence scoring

  • 1 source type: Hypothesis (monitor)
  • 2 source types: Emerging pattern (investigate)
  • 3+ source types: Robust pattern (act on)
  • Diverse sources > volume from one method
  • AI + human synthesis outperforms either alone (Torres): use AI as thought partner, not replacement

Rhythm

  • Bi-weekly synthesis sessions
  • Monthly pattern review
  • Quarterly trajectory update

Key discipline

  • Resist premature closure
  • Look for disconfirming evidence
  • Update trajectories, don't just add
  • Name patterns vividly
3
Layer 03

Insight Crystallisation

The interpretive layer
Where patterns get meaning. Every insight must articulate the tension it reveals — tensions are what make insights actionable. "What's happening" becomes "what it means for people and why."
↓ Click to expand

Insight anatomy

  • Observation: What we see happening
  • Underlying driver: Why (human truth)
  • Tension: Unresolved pull between competing needs
  • Supporting patterns: From Layer 2
  • Confidence & maturity

Tension archetypes

  • Want vs. Constraint
  • Promise vs. Reality
  • Identity vs. Behaviour
  • Old norm vs. Emerging norm

Rhythm

  • Monthly insight workshops
  • Quarterly insight library refresh
  • Annual tension landscape review

Key discipline

  • Insight without tension = observation
  • Could a competitor use this differently?
  • Write for a strategist
  • Track tension evolution over time
4
Layer 04

Implication Mapping

The strategic translation
Where insight meets business context. The same insight has radically different implications depending on position, capabilities, and ambitions. This layer asks: what options does this open or close?
↓ Click to expand

Implication anatomy

  • Source insight: Linked from Layer 3
  • Decision domain: Brand, innovation, pricing, channel, comms, CX
  • Strategic options: What this opens
  • Strategic risks: What this closes
  • Urgency: Act now / Plan for / Monitor

Decision domains

  • Brand positioning & identity
  • Innovation pipeline & NPD
  • Pricing architecture
  • Channel & distribution
  • Communication & media
  • Customer experience
  • Organisational capability

Rhythm

  • Monthly workshops with strategy
  • Quarterly roadmap alignment
  • Ad hoc for urgent signals

Key discipline

  • Always name the trade-off
  • Present options, not conclusions
  • Map to specific decisions
  • Us vs. category implications
5
Layer 05

Decision Activation

The spine itself
The registry of decisions — past, pending, and anticipated. Each carries its own anatomy: trigger, evidence, tensions navigated, bet placed, outcome observed. The loop closes here.
↓ Click to expand

Decision record

  • Trigger: Why now?
  • Question: What specifically?
  • Evidence base: Linked + convergence scores
  • Tensions navigated: Trade-offs accepted
  • The bet: Falsifiable hypothesis

Feedback loop

  • Expected outcome: If right, what & when?
  • Feedback triggers: What signals to watch
  • Kill criteria: What makes us reverse?
  • Actual outcome → feeds Layer 1

Rhythm

  • Logged at commitment point
  • Monthly feedback trigger review
  • Quarterly outcome audit
  • Annual decision quality review

Key discipline

  • Record before you know the outcome
  • Be explicit about the bet
  • Distinguish wrong decision vs. poor execution
  • Close the loop — always
§2 — Connective Mechanisms
What makes this connected,
not just organised.
↺

Threading

Any element at any layer can link to any other, through many-to-many relationships (Pidcock, Atomic Research). A signal can jump to a decision if urgent. An implication can challenge an old pattern. One fact feeds multiple insights; multiple insights ground one recommendation. The system is a web, not a funnel.

↔

Tension Tracking

Tensions persist for years. New signals add nuance, new patterns shift shape, multiple decisions attempt resolution. This reveals whether the business addresses root dynamics or treats symptoms.

◴

Temporal Tagging

Every element carries a relevance horizon. Cultural macro-trend: 3–5 years. Competitive move: 6 months. Behavioural signal: 2–3 month leading indicator.

§4 — Constraints & Blindspots
Where each element fails in practice.

Every element has failure modes — most invisible to the person inside the decision. Click to reveal the constraint, blindspot, and stress test.

01
The Trigger

What you think prompted the decision is rarely the real reason

+
Core blindspot

The stated trigger is the respectable one. But decisions are more often triggered by fear, internal politics, sunk cost escalation, or mimicry. Teams rarely interrogate honestly — so the decision gets optimised for the wrong problem.

Deeper constraint

Urgency distorts evidence. When a trigger creates time pressure, teams reach for what's available rather than what's relevant. The most dangerous decisions feel urgent but aren't.

Stress test

"If this trigger had appeared two years ago, would we have acted? If not — what changed: the evidence, or our anxiety?"

02
The Question

Framing is the invisible decision before the decision

+
Core blindspot

How you frame the question pre-selects the answer. "Should we enter this category?" hides the real decision, which might be "what's the least risky way to learn if this category is viable?" The question is usually inherited and rarely re-examined.

Deeper constraint

Questions carry embedded assumptions. "How do we win with Gen Z?" assumes Gen Z is a meaningful segment, that "winning" means the same thing, and that the current model can accommodate whatever the answer is.

Stress test

"Rewrite the question three different ways. The version that makes you most uncomfortable is probably closest to the real decision."

03
The Evidence Base

You will always find evidence for what you already believe

+
Confirmation curation

Confirmation bias operates at selection, not interpretation. Teams don't distort evidence — they curate it. Three supporting signals get cited; seven complicating ones get filed as "interesting but not actionable."

Survivorship bias

Your insight library only contains what past research investigated. Unstudied categories are invisible, not absent. The most important insight might be in research you didn't commission.

The streetlight effect

Teams look where measurement is easy, not where the answer lives. Digital data is abundant and precise so it gets over-indexed. Cultural shifts get treated as "soft" even when they're the actual driver.

Recency bias

The most recent research dominates regardless of relevance. A three-year-old segmentation might be more pertinent than last month's concept test.

Fragment evidence

Support tickets, feature requests, and analytics fragments are treated as complete evidence. Torres warns these lack the full story: "we tend to get fragments... we don't know the whole story." Treat non-interview sources as inspiration for investigation, not as findings to act on directly.

The absent field

No field for "what evidence would change our mind?" Without this, the evidence base functions as prosecution, not investigation.

Stress test

"What would we need to see to abandon this direction? If no one can answer, the evidence base is a costume, not a foundation."

04
Tensions Navigated

The tension you don't name is the one that kills you

+
Core blindspot

Records capture what was gained, never what was sacrificed. Twelve months later, when the sacrificed dimension causes problems, nobody remembers it was a conscious choice. It's treated as failure rather than trade-off.

Undiscussable tensions

"What the data says" vs. "what the CEO believes" is real but won't appear in any document. "Right long-term" vs. "hits this quarter's targets" is equally real and equally absent.

Depth vs. decisiveness

More research always feels safer, but delayed action is invisible. There's no line item for "six months spent being certain while the market moved."

Stress test

"What will we be worse at because of this decision? If the answer is 'nothing' — you haven't found the real trade-off."

05
The Bet

Most organisations cannot articulate their hypothesis

+
Core blindspot

"Betting on premiumisation" is a direction, not a hypothesis. A hypothesis is falsifiable: "Consumers will pay 20% more for verifiable transparency because trust is the binding purchase constraint." Directions can't be tested.

Accountability avoidance

Explicit bets create accountability. Organisations avoid this instinctively. A vague bet protects everyone — if strategy fails, the hypothesis was never clear enough to falsify, so failure gets attributed to execution.

Stress test

"Complete: 'We believe [who] will [what] because [why], verified by [metric] by [date].' If you can't — you don't have a bet."

06
Expected Outcome

Precision creates the illusion of prediction

+
False specificity

"15% uplift in 6 months" sounds rigorous but is usually reverse-engineered from the business case, not predicted from evidence. The expected outcome becomes a negotiated fiction.

Multi-speed outcomes

Decisions have outcome dimensions moving at different speeds. Teams pick the fastest metric (validates sooner) which may be the least meaningful. Separate leading indicators from lagging indicators.

Stress test

"Where did this number come from — the evidence, or the business case? If you can't trace it to data or precedent, it's a wish."

07
Feedback Triggers

The signals you pre-commit to are never the ones that matter most

+
Confirming triggers

Triggers are set at maximum confidence — right after the decision. They skew toward confirming signals. A real system needs kill criteria: "If X happens, we reverse." Almost no organisation defines these in advance.

Biased interpreters

If the champion monitors their own decision, interpretation is biased. Feedback should ideally be monitored by someone with no stake — which is organisationally almost impossible.

Signal vs. noise

The interval between decision and feedback is filled with noise mistaken for signal. A post-launch spike might be distribution, not response. Waiting for real data is the hardest discipline.

Stress test

"What would make us stop? If nobody defined kill criteria before launch, we're cheerleading, not monitoring."

08
Actual Outcome

Attribution is the final lie

+
False attribution

Did it succeed because the hypothesis was right, or because the market moved favourably for unrelated reasons? Did it fail because the bet was wrong, execution was poor, or a competitor did something unpredictable?

Narrative capture

Organisations need simple stories. "It worked because consumers rewarded honesty" is clean. "It coincided with a competitor's scandal" is inconvenient. The simple narrative wins, teaching the wrong lesson.

Stress test

"Name three alternative explanations for the outcome. If you can't, you're storytelling, not analysing."

§5 — Structural Blindspots
Five ways the architecture itself can fail.

Beyond individual elements, the system has systemic vulnerabilities. These aren't flaws to fix — they're tensions to manage.

◌

The Negative Space

The most consequential blindspot is what never enters the system — questions not asked, segments not studied, shifts not tracked because they don't map to the current category.

AntidoteQuarterly "negative space audit." What's adjacent that we've decided isn't relevant? Rotate an outsider through signal capture.
↺

Power in Interpretation

Whoever controls the narrative at Layer 3 controls decisions at Layer 5. A senior person's gut feel overrides careful analysis, and the framework has no defence.

AntidoteSeparate insight workshop from implication workshop. Let insights crystallise before strategy enters the room.
∞

The Accumulation Problem

Over time, the system generates more than anyone can hold. Without curation, it becomes an archive. Old patterns persist and distort new interpretation. Atomic Research practitioners report the same: redundant entries accumulate, taxonomy sprawls, and scale introduces complexity that low-code tools alone cannot manage (Synopsis).

AntidoteBuild deprecation into the rhythm. Retire as many insights as promoted. Auto-archive after 6 months without update. Regular taxonomy audits.
⊕

The Adoption Failure

Building the system is the easy part. Getting people to use it is the hard part. Sharon (Atomic Research) built a repository at WeWork that housed 4,000+ nuggets, but practitioners consistently report that adoption is a change management challenge, not a tooling problem. Anderson built a repository that nobody touched despite enthusiastic launch demos.

AntidoteInvolve future users in taxonomy design. Test the system's usability. Treat adoption as a cultural shift, not a rollout. Start with one team, one decision, one cycle.
♯

The Rationality Assumption

The framework assumes evidence-based deliberation. Many decisions are emotional or political, then retrospectively fitted with evidence.

AntidoteComplete the decision record before the outcome is known. The gap between stated and actual reasoning becomes harder to hide.
§6 — Decision Quality Audit
Pre-flight checklist.

Run before any significant decision. Each question surfaces a specific blindspot. If you can't answer honestly, the decision isn't ready.

✓

Trigger Integrity

Have we named the real trigger — including fear, politics, or sunk-cost pressure?

✓

Question Reframe

Written the question three ways? Did the uncomfortable version make it into the room?

✓

Disconfirming Evidence

Can we name evidence arguing against this? Has it been weighed, not just acknowledged?

✓

Convergence Quality

3+ independent source types in the evidence base? Or just volume from one method?

✓

Streetlight Check

Over-indexing on what's easy to measure? A harder-to-measure dimension might be the real driver.

✓

Negative Space

What haven't we researched that could change this? What adjacent territory assumed irrelevant?

✓

Tension Honesty

Named what we'll be worse at? Including the undiscussable tensions?

✓

Falsifiable Bet

"We believe [who] will [what] because [why], by [metric] by [date]." Completeable?

✓

Evidence-Based Expectations

Expected outcomes traced to data — or reverse-engineered from the business case?

✓

Leading vs. Lagging

Separated metrics that move first (may mislead) from those that move last (probably real)?

✓

Kill Criteria Defined

Pre-committed conditions for reversal? Who monitors — and are they independent?

✓

Independent Monitor

Feedback trigger owner different from decision champion?

✓

Pre-mortem Complete

12 months out, this failed — most likely reason? Is it addressed in the plan?

✓

Record Before Outcome

Full decision record written down before we know what happens?

✓

Reverse Decision Tree

Can we trace every piece of evidence back to a real source? (Torres: "Did we hear that from a customer? Did it come from a stakeholder?") Where are the gaps?

✓

Evidence Chain Integrity

Can we walk from the recommendation back through insights to facts to original sources? (Pidcock, Atomic Research: you cannot create a recommendation without insights, and you cannot create insights without facts.) Any broken links?
