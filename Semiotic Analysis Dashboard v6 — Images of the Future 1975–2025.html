Images of the Future

A semiotic analysis of how science fiction imagines the future across 6,877 cultural artifacts spanning 1975–2025. Examining signs, myths, codes, and human truths across movies, novels, TV shows, games, and more—from the Techno-Progressive Frontier of Era 1 to the Managed decline of Era 2, across 5 cultural traditions.

The Core Finding: From Frontier to Managed Decline—Across 6,877 artifacts and fifty years, the dominant image of the future has shifted from an expansive frontier rewarding courage to a declining system demanding endurance. Space exploration drops from 56% to 35%. Alien encounters halve. Corporate power doubles. Identity crisis rises by 38%. The future is no longer a destination to reach but a condition to survive.

The Selective Tradition: Williams argued that each era selectively preserves some codes while suppressing others. Across the full corpus, Dominant codes account for 65.8% of all works (rising from 64.3% in Era 1 to 67.0% in Era 2). Residual codes decline from 16.7% to 13.1%. What gets preserved reveals what the culture needs to believe; what gets discarded reveals what it cannot face. Climate Fiction leads the emergent codes (1,082 works total), yet Solarpunk remains marginal (178 works)—imagining a world where human intention matters at scale is the hardest imaginative act of all.

Human Truths

The unconscious contradictions driving science fiction

The Five Human Truths

1
Compensatory: "We have lost the ability to imagine collective transformation"
The absence of successful collective political transformation in 6,877 artifacts is not a limitation of imagination but a statement about what we unconsciously believe is possible. This absence compensates for the real political defeats of the left in the neoliberal era. Fiction tells us what we secretly think is achievable.
We dream of individual transcendence because we have surrendered on collective change.
2
Transition: "The question 'Who am I?' has replaced 'What kind of world do we want?'"
Identity Crisis rises from 13.9% to 17.7% of all works (+3.8%). Memory Manipulation rises from 16.5% to 18.1%. These are not expansions of consciousness but retreats into the self. When collective futures close, consciousness contracts into the only remaining frontier: the self.
Endless self-examination feels like freedom because the alternative—collective action—has become unthinkable.
3
Taboo: "We are more afraid of ourselves than of anything the universe might contain"
Alien Contact declines from 22.8% to 15.6% (−7.2%). Space Exploration drops from 32.2% to 24.2% (−8.0%). The unknown external other becomes less threatening as the unknown internal self—fragmenting, replicating, uncontrollable—becomes the true anxiety. The outer frontier closes; the inner frontier opens.
The universe is safer than consciousness. Outer space is less terrifying than inner space.
4
Contradiction: "We imagine struggle more easily than we imagine transformation"
Heroic Struggle dominates both eras—47% in Era 1, intensifying to 53% in Era 2. Meanwhile, Providential narratives (faith that larger forces will deliver us) collapse from 8% to just 3%. Dystopian visions rise from 19.5% to 25.2%, while Utopian visions hold flat at ~3.5%. We can imagine fighting the world but not changing its fundamental structure.
Struggle paradoxically feels more realistic than transformation because it does not require imagining a fundamentally different system.
5
Scarcity: "The Unknown is overtaking the Known as the default condition of the future"
The Known-vs-Unknown binary shifts dramatically: Known drops from 31.3% to 18.1%, while Unknown surges from 22.9% to 36.0%. Isolation and Loneliness rise from 9.2% to 10.7%. We represent ourselves as lost—not in hostile territory, but in uncharted waters where the old maps have dissolved.
We have built systems of unprecedented information access and are becoming increasingly uncertain. The data was supposed to solve this problem.
Foresight Implications: Across 6,877 artifacts, these five truths are not about the future—they are about the present. Heroic Struggle at 53% tells us we believe the future is worth fighting for but not worth reimagining. The collapse of Providential faith (8% → 3%) tells us we no longer trust benevolent forces. The surge of the Unknown (22.9% → 36.0%) tells us we have lost our maps. If we want to change the images of the future, we must first acknowledge these underlying beliefs. The work is not imaginative; it is political.

The Image of the Future in 2025

Across 6,877 cultural artifacts spanning half a century and five cultural traditions, a single pattern emerges with striking clarity: the future has not become simply darker — it has become a place where struggle is the default mode of existence.

Heroic Struggle dominates both eras — 47% in Era 1, intensifying to 53% in Era 2. This is not dystopia. Dystopia implies a system that has already won. Heroic Struggle implies a system worth fighting. The distinction matters: our fiction tells us we have not surrendered. But it also tells us we cannot imagine victory. Transformative narratives hold steady at 25%, never growing. We can picture the fight. We cannot picture what comes after.

What collapses is faith. Providential narratives — the belief that something larger than human agency will intervene — drop from 8% to 3%. No god, no benevolent alien, no evolutionary breakthrough will save us. The Faustian connotation of technology more than doubles (4.3% → 10.6%): technology is no longer Promethean gift but bargain with uncertain terms. The Known gives way to the Unknown (31.3% → 18.1% vs 22.9% → 36.0%) — the most dramatic binary shift in the entire corpus. We have more information than any civilisation in history and less certainty about what it means.

The signifiers that rise tell a story of inward retreat: Identity Crisis (+3.8%), Memory Manipulation (+1.6%), Isolation (+1.5%). Those that decline tell a story of closing frontiers: Space Exploration (−8.0%), Alien Contact (−7.2%), Body Horror (−4.1%). The outer frontier closes; the inner frontier opens. Managed Apocalypse more than doubles as a primary myth (5.8% → 12.2%). The future is not somewhere we go. It is something we maintain.

Yet the data resists pure pessimism. Hope as a binary pole rises (12.8% → 19.3%) even as Despair also grows (11.8% → 14.6%). The middle — the Balanced position — contracts, from 75.4% to 66.0%. Ambiguity gives way to polarisation. Individual Heroes resurge as primary agents (18.1% → 21.1%). Hybrid Agency — the fusion of human and technological action — emerges from near-zero (2.3%) to a significant force (9.8%). Across five traditions, the Japanese, Korean, and Chinese canons increasingly offer alternative models of agency, myth, and future vision that challenge Western dominance.

Fred Polak argued that a civilisation's vitality can be measured by the vigour and realism of its images of the future. By that measure, the image of the future in 2025 is neither dying nor thriving — it is hardening. It is becoming more combative, more uncertain, more individual, and more aware of the price of its own technologies. The question is not whether the images are optimistic or pessimistic. The question is whether they can imagine change at scale — and on that count, the silence across 6,877 artifacts is deafening.

Glossary

Key terms and frameworks from semiotic analysis

Glossary of Terms

Signifier / Signified
Saussure's fundamental distinction. The signifier is the word or image (the form); the signified is the concept or meaning (the content). "Spaceship" as signifier can signify hope, conquest, escape, or doom depending on context.
Denotation / Connotation
Denotation is the literal meaning (a spaceship is a vehicle for space travel). Connotation is the cultural baggage (a spaceship connotes freedom, progress, or folly depending on era and context).
Myth (Barthes)
A second-order sign system. It takes cultural meanings (progress, heroism, technology) and naturalizes them as universal truths. Myths hide history; they present contingent social arrangements as inevitable.
Residual / Dominant / Emergent
Williams' framework for understanding cultural forms at any historical moment. Residual codes are from earlier periods and still active. Dominant codes are the system's core. Emergent codes are new, not yet incorporated into the system.
Image of the Future (Polak)
The culturally shared vision of what the future could or will be. Polak argued that civilizations with vivid, desirable, achievable future images are vital and can mobilize energy. Civilizations that imagine only decline lose their animating principle.
Binary Opposition
A fundamental structure in Western thought where meaning is created through opposition (nature/culture, male/female, reason/emotion). SF reveals which side of the binary is privileged in any era.
Iconogenesis
The creation of iconic images that crystallize a culture's imagination. Examples: the Spaceship from Era 1, the Algorithm from Era 2.
Iconoclasm
The destruction or breaking of icons. When a culture's foundational images lose power (Alien Contact declines; Frontier closes), it represents an iconoclastic moment.
Iconoplasm
The transformation of icons into new forms. When the Frontier icon doesn't disappear but transforms into Managed Decline, that's iconoplasm.
Capitalist Realism
Mark Fisher's term for the widespread sense that there is no alternative to capitalism. SF reveals this as the dominant unconscious belief of our era—we can imagine the apocalypse, but not the end of markets.
Used Future
SF futures that are weathered, worn, functional rather than sleek and utopian. Dominant in recent SF. Signals an era that assumes the future will be patched, compromised, degraded.
Disowned Future
Futures we are unable or unwilling to imagine. In this dataset, the disowned future is collective transformation. It is systematically absent—which reveals our deepest political unconscious.
Image-Working
The active work of imagining alternatives. If the current set of future images is limiting or tragic, image-working is the conscious practice of generating new ones. It is political work.
The Breach (Polak)
The space between present and future. A large breach requires transformation; a small breach is mere optimization. The breach has contracted in recent decades, narrowing what futures seem possible.
